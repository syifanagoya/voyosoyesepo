Cesar Chavez (born César Estrada Chávez, locally: [sesa estaa taes]; March 31, 1927 – April 23, 1993) was an American farm worker, labour leader, and civil rights activist who co-founded the National Farm Workers Association (later the United Farm Workers union, UFW) with Dolores Huerta. His public-relations approach to unionism, along with strong but nonviolent methods, elevated the agricultural workers’ cause to a moral cause with widespread support. By the late 1970s, his efforts had persuaded farmers in California and Florida to accept the UFW as the negotiating representative for 50,000 field workers.

 The UFW’s membership had fallen to about 15,000 by the mid-1980s. During his lifetime, Colegio Cesar Chavez was one of the few institutions named after him, but following his death, he became a significant historical figure for the Latino community, with numerous schools, streets, and parks bearing his name. Since then, he has become a symbol of organised labour and leftist politics, representing support for workers and Hispanic empowerment via grassroots organisation.

 He is also known for popularising the phrase “S, se puede” (Spanish for “Yes, one can” or, approximately, “Yes, it can be done”), which was used by Barack Obama during his 2008 presidential campaign. His supporters claim that his efforts resulted in many benefits for union workers. Despite the fact that the UFW collapsed after a few years, Chavez became a famous “folk saint” in the pantheon of Mexican Americans following his death in 199Cesar Chavez Day has been declared a state holiday in California, Colorado, and Texas on his birthday, March 31.
